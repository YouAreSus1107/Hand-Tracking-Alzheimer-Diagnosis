## 1. Why the Hand?

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is defined primarily as a *cognitive* disease, yet the brain regions governing higher cognition overlap heavily with regions governing voluntary and automatic motor function. The motor cortex, basal ganglia, and cerebellum form an interconnected circuit responsible for:

- **Voluntary movement** — motor cortex + basal ganglia
- **Automatic/learned movement** — basal ganglia (procedural memory)
- **Coordination, rhythm, timing** — cerebellum
- **Sensory integration** (proprioceptive, tactile, vestibular, visual) — cerebellum

The hand is uniquely sensitive because fine motor control demands *all of the above simultaneously*. A simple act like tapping a finger requires:
- Cortical motor planning
- Basal ganglia automaticity loops
- Cerebellar timing and error correction
- Sensory feedback integration

When AD begins to erode neurons — particularly in the association cortices and basal ganglia — the degradation is **observable in hand movement** before clinical cognitive symptoms are salient enough to diagnose. In short: **the hand is a peripheral readout of central neurodegeneration.**

The paper's central thesis is that hand dexterity is "strongly correlated with cognitive function in all 17 studies reviewed" and that specific hand movement parameters can serve as accessible, low-cost early biomarkers.

### 2.2 Longitudinal Studies — Predictive Power

#### Darweesh et al. (2017) — The Largest Study
- **N = 4,856** community-dwelling individuals followed for **9.2 years**
- Used the **Purdue Pegboard Test (PPT)** at baseline
- Outcome: 227 dementia cases and 50 Parkinson's cases observed during follow-up
- **Key finding: Every 1-unit decrease in average PPT score correlated with a 28% increased risk of developing degenerative disease**
- Effect was stronger in individuals **under age 70**, suggesting early measurement is critical
- Conclusion: Low finger dexterity in midlife predicts AD before any cognitive symptom appears

#### Beeri et al. (2021) — Grip Strength as Independent Predictor
- **N = 1,160** (580 African American, 580 European American)
- Measurement: Jamar hand dynamometer for grip strength
- **Key finding: Each 1 standard deviation increase in global motor function reduced AD risk by ~20%**
- Of all motor metrics tested (dexterity, gait speed, balance), grip strength was the **only motor component independently associated with incident AD dementia** after controlling for confounders
- Result held across both racial groups, indicating robustness

#### Liou et al. (2020) — Functional ADL Motor Tasks
- **N = 32,818** (11,935 mild AD; 20,883 moderate-to-severe AD)
- Tasks: pen-holding, buttoning, knotting
- Moderate-to-severe AD significantly worse than mild AD on all three tasks
- **Key finding: Fine motor skill decline significantly predicts transition to severe dementia**
- Implication: Functional motor tasks can stage dementia progression

#### de Paula et al. (2016) — Discriminating the Spectrum
- **N = 124** across four groups: Healthy Elderly (HE), amnestic MCI (aMCI), multi-domain MCI, and AD
- Nine-hole pegboard test performance:
  - **HE vs. AD: p < 0.001**
  - **aMCI vs. AD: p = 0.004**
  - **HE vs. multi-domain MCI: p = 0.009**
  - HE vs. aMCI: *not significant* (simple tests insufficient)
- Hierarchy: HE > aMCI > multi-domain MCI > AD
- **Critical insight: Simple pegboard tests cannot reliably distinguish healthy aging from MCI — complex movement tasks are required**

### 2.3 Experimental Studies — What Parameters Matter

#### Movement Velocity and Its Variability (Schroter et al., 2003)
- 40 healthy, 35 AD, 39 MCI, 39 depression on a **circle-drawing tablet task**
- AD patients showed:
  - Greater **variation in speed (V-SD)** — the *inconsistency* of speed, not just average speed
  - Greater **relative velocity variation (V-Rel)**
  - Significantly less **automated, accurate, and regular** movements
- This is the key insight: AD doesn't just make you slow — it makes you **irregularly slow**, losing the automaticity that healthy brains execute without effort

#### Finger Tapping Timing and Rhythm (Suzumura et al., 2016/18/21)
Multiple studies using finger-tapping devices with magnetic sensors on the fingertip:
- Healthy subjects: **regular distance/speed** in repetitive tapping
- AD/MCI subjects: **irregular patterns**, with deterioration correlating to MMSE score
- **Bimanual tasks** (requiring independent vs. dependent hand movements): AD showed greater difficulty than single-hand tasks, suggesting bimanual coordination is especially sensitive
- **Contact duration** with the tapping surface: Longer and more irregular in AD
- As AD progresses: agility ↓, reaction speed ↓, rhythm ↓, coordination ↓
- **Non-dominant hand parameters showed the strongest correlation with MMSE scores** — this is a critical finding for test design

#### Finger Tapping Count and Intra-Individual Variability (Roalf et al., 2018)
- 302 subjects: 62 healthy, 131 AD, 46 MCI, 63 Parkinson's disease
- Light-beam finger tapping device:
  - **Total taps per interval**: decreased in AD and MCI vs. healthy
  - **Inter-tap interval (ITI) speed**: longer in AD and MCI
  - **Intra-individual variability (IIV) of ITI**: elevated in all neurodegenerative groups
  - Parkinson's had the highest IIV; AD and MCI had intermediate
- **Key finding: IIV alone can differentiate Parkinson's from AD/MCI**
- Implication: The *variability signature* of tapping is disease-specific

#### Handwriting Analysis (Yu et al., 2019)
- Computerized analysis of Chinese character writing on tablet
- AD and MCI vs. healthy controls:
  - **Larger multi-scale entropy (MSE)** — more chaotic/irregular writing dynamics
  - **Larger pause time per stroke (PTS)** — longer hesitations mid-stroke
  - **Larger ratio of air-to-paper time (RAPL)** — more time pen hovering vs. writing
  - **Larger character size** with **lower spatial accuracy** — scaling dysregulation
- Even MCI (pre-AD) showed detectable differences in handwriting dynamics

#### Spiral Drawing — Velocity, Pressure, Altitude (Kachouri et al., 2021)
- Archimedes spiral drawing on digitizing tablet:
  - **65.7% of healthy controls showed high-pressure values vs. AD patients**
  - **48% of AD patients showed very low velocity vs. 30% in healthy controls**
  - Motion becomes progressively **slower from early disease**
- The spiral is a sensitive instrument because it requires continuous, smooth, spatially precise motor control — exactly what AD disrupts

#### Typing / Keystroke Analysis (Van Waes et al., 2017)
- Keystroke logging during a typing copy test
- Healthy elderly already type slower than adolescents
- MCI and AD show **further degraded keystroke timing and rhythm**
- "Typing copy test provides valuable diagnostic information"
- Advantage: completely naturalistic, no specialized equipment

## 3. The Biomarker Framework

Based on the 17 studies, the following parameters constitute measurable hand-based biomarkers for AD:

### Category A — Timing/Speed Parameters
| Biomarker | What It Measures | Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|
| Mean tap interval | Average speed of repetitive tapping | Moderate |
| Tap rate (taps/sec) | Overall motor speed | Moderate |
| Inter-tap interval (ITI) | Time between successive taps | High |
| Intra-individual variability (IIV) | Consistency of ITI | Very High |
| Pegboard completion time | Manual dexterity speed | Moderate |
| Air time ratio | Time spent hovering vs. executing | High |

### Category B — Movement Quality Parameters
| Biomarker | What It Measures | Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|
| Velocity variability (V-SD) | Smoothness/automaticity of motion | Very High |
| Relative velocity (V-Rel) | Proportional speed regularity | High |
| Spatial accuracy | Precision of endpoint targeting | High |
| Movement entropy (MSE) | Complexity/chaos in motion | High |
| Contact duration | Time maintaining contact at target | High |

### Category C — Functional/Composite Parameters
| Biomarker | What It Measures | Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|
| Grip strength | Overall hand motor capacity | Very High |
| Bimanual coordination | Independent vs. dependent hand control | Very High |
| Non-dominant hand performance | Contralateral hemisphere integrity | Very High |
| ADL task performance | Pen-holding, buttoning, knotting | Moderate |

### Category D — Distinguishing Diagnostic Utility
| Comparison | Best Test Type |
|---|---|
| Healthy vs. AD | Simple pegboard, grip strength |
| Healthy vs. MCI | Complex handwriting, spiral drawing |
| MCI vs. AD | IIV, velocity variability, bimanual coordination |
| AD vs. Parkinson's | IIV signature (Parkinson's higher) |
| Mild AD vs. Severe AD | ADL fine motor tasks |

## 4. Applying This to the 3D Hand Tracking System

The `hand_tracking.py` system uses **MediaPipe Hand Landmarker** to capture **21 landmarks in 3D (x, y, z)** from a standard webcam in real time. Each landmark is a joint or landmark on the hand:

```
Landmark Map:
  0: Wrist
  1-4: Thumb (CMC → MCP → IP → TIP)
  5-8: Index (MCP → PIP → DIP → TIP)
  9-12: Middle (MCP → PIP → DIP → TIP)
  13-16: Ring (MCP → PIP → DIP → TIP)
  17-20: Pinky (MCP → PIP → DIP → TIP)
```

The system currently captures coordinates at ~30 fps (webcam rate), with both left and right hands tracked independently. This gives a **time series of 3D joint positions** — exactly the raw material needed to compute the biomarkers above.

### 4.1 What the System Can Directly Measure

#### Finger Tapping Test (→ Suzumura, Roalf)
Track the distance between **Landmark 8 (index fingertip)** and **Landmark 4 (thumb tip)**. When this distance crosses a threshold, a "tap" is registered.

From the tap event series, compute:
- **Tap rate** — taps per 10 seconds
- **Inter-tap interval (ITI)** — time between successive taps in milliseconds
- **IIV** — standard deviation of ITI values; the single most sensitive AD biomarker in the literature
- **Contact duration** — frames spent with fingertip-to-thumb distance below threshold

This directly replicates the magnetic sensor finger-tapping studies (Suzumura et al.) and light-beam tapping studies (Roalf et al.).

#### Velocity and Velocity Variability (→ Schroter)
For any landmark (particularly Landmark 8 or 0), compute:
- **Frame-to-frame velocity** = Euclidean distance between positions at time *t* and *t+1*, divided by frame interval
- **V-SD** = standard deviation of velocity over a motion segment
- **V-Rel** = V-SD / mean velocity (coefficient of variation of speed)

These are precisely the metrics Schroter et al. used to distinguish AD from healthy controls in circle-drawing tasks. High V-SD and V-Rel indicate loss of motor automaticity.

#### Jerk (Derivative of Acceleration)
Compute the third derivative of landmark position over time:
```
velocity = Δposition / Δt
acceleration = Δvelocity / Δt
jerk = Δacceleration / Δt
```
The **mean squared jerk** normalized by movement amplitude is a measure of movement smoothness. Healthy, automatic movements have low jerk; AD-affected movements have high jerk due to irregular speed profiles.

#### Spatial Accuracy (→ Yu et al.)
Define a 3D target point in space. Ask the patient to repeatedly touch that point with their fingertip. Measure:
- **Mean endpoint error** — average distance from landmark 8 to target on each reach
- **Endpoint variability** — standard deviation of endpoint positions across trials
- This mimics the spatial accuracy deficit found in handwriting and spiral tasks

#### Bimanual Coordination (→ Suzumura)
With both hands tracked (now implemented), measure:
- **Phase relationship** between equivalent landmarks on left and right hand during symmetric tasks
- **Cross-correlation** of the two hands' velocity profiles
- **Phase jitter** — variability in the lead/lag timing between hands
- A healthy brain executes symmetric bimanual movements with high phase consistency; AD disrupts this

#### Air Time vs. Active Movement Ratio (→ Yu et al.)
Analogous to the handwriting air-to-paper ratio: define "active" as when the hand is moving above a velocity threshold, and "idle" as when it is stationary or below threshold. Track the ratio of idle time to total task time.

#### Spiral Tracing in 3D Space (→ Kachouri et al.)
Render a virtual Archimedes spiral as a UI overlay. Ask the patient to trace it with their index fingertip in the air. Measure:
- **Deviation from the ideal spiral path** (mean distance from landmark 8 to nearest spiral point)
- **Velocity along the path** and its variability
- Directly replicates Kachouri et al.'s pressure/velocity spiral findings

#### Non-Dominant Hand Analysis (→ Suzumura)
Since the system labels packets as `L:[...]` or `R:[...]`, dominant vs. non-dominant hand metrics can be computed separately. Non-dominant hand parameters correlated most strongly with MMSE scores — therefore non-dominant hand IIV and V-SD may be the most sensitive single measures.

> **Note on grip strength:** The camera cannot measure force. However, grip *posture*, speed of gripping, and hand closure regularity (tracking wrist + all finger landmarks during a fist-closing motion) can serve as proxy metrics. True grip strength measurement would require hardware (dynamometer).

### 4.3 3D Depth as a Unique Advantage

Most prior studies used 2D tablets, pegboards, or light-beam tappers. The `z` coordinate provided by MediaPipe (depth relative to the wrist) enables:

- **Tremor detection** in the depth axis (involuntary oscillation in z)
- **Movement plane assessment** — whether motion is confined to a normal plane or shows pathological deviation
- **True 3D spatial accuracy** — not just 2D endpoint error
- **Finger independence** — measuring whether each finger's z-movement is truly independent (important for bimanual independence tests)

This gives a richer signal than the 2D instruments used in most reviewed studies.

## 5. Proposed Diagnostic Test Protocol

A standardized assessment session using the 3D hand tracking system could include the following tasks. Each maps directly to validated biomarkers in the literature.

### Test 1: Index Finger Tapping (60 seconds)
**Instruction:** "Tap your index finger to your thumb as regularly as possible."
**Measurement:**
- Tap count, tap rate, ITI, IIV
- V-SD and V-Rel of the tapping motion
- Contact duration per tap

**Expected finding:** AD patients show elevated IIV and V-SD; MCI shows intermediate values relative to healthy elderly (Suzumura, Roalf).

### Test 2: Sequential Finger-to-Thumb (4 × 15 seconds)
**Instruction:** "Touch each finger to your thumb in sequence: index, middle, ring, pinky. Repeat."
**Measurement:**
- Sequencing speed
- Error rate (out-of-order touches)
- Rhythm regularity
- Automaticity (does speed increase with repetition as learned?)

**Expected finding:** Loss of automaticity in AD; healthy subjects show speed increase over time, AD patients do not.

### Test 3: Non-Dominant Hand Tapping (30 seconds)
**Same as Test 1 but non-dominant hand only.**
**Rationale:** Non-dominant hand parameters are the strongest MMSE correlates (Suzumura et al.).

### Test 4: Bimanual Symmetric Tapping (30 seconds)
**Instruction:** "Tap both index fingers to their respective thumbs at the same time."
**Measurement:**
- Phase coherence between left and right hand taps
- Phase jitter (variability in synchrony)
- IIV for each hand independently

**Expected finding:** AD patients show greater phase jitter and bimanual asynchrony (Suzumura bimanual findings).

### Test 5: Air Spiral Tracing (45 seconds)
**Instruction:** Show patient an Archimedes spiral overlay on screen. Ask them to trace it with their index fingertip.
**Measurement:**
- Path deviation (mean distance from ideal spiral)
- Velocity variability along path
- Air time / active time ratio

**Expected finding:** AD patients show slower, more irregular tracing with higher path deviation (Kachouri).

### Test 6: 3D Reach-and-Return (20 trials)
**Instruction:** Display a 3D target point. Patient touches it and returns to rest.
**Measurement:**
- Endpoint accuracy
- Movement time
- Variability across trials

**Expected finding:** AD patients show higher endpoint variability and longer movement time.

## 6. Severity Staging Using Hand Metrics

Based on the longitudinal studies, a rough progression-linked framework can be constructed:

| Stage | Expected Hand Metrics |
|---|---|
| **Healthy** | Low IIV (<50ms), low V-SD, high tap rate (>4/sec), precise spatial targeting, good bimanual coherence |
| **Subtle/Preclinical** | Slightly elevated IIV, marginal V-SD increase, detectable only on complex tasks |
| **MCI** | IIV elevated, reduced tap rate, measurable loss of automaticity in sequential tasks, handwriting air-time increased |
| **Mild AD** | Clear IIV elevation, significant V-SD, bimanual phase jitter evident, spatial accuracy degraded, sequential task errors |
| **Moderate-to-Severe AD** | Marked decline in all metrics, inability to complete sequential tasks, severe bimanual decoupling |

The paper found that the transition from **MCI to AD** is best detected by **complex movement parameters** (handwriting, IIV, bimanual coordination) rather than simple pegboard-equivalent tests — directly informing which of the six tests above should receive diagnostic weight.

## 7. Neurological Basis of the Observable Signals

Understanding *why* these signals appear helps interpret measurements correctly.

**Loss of Automaticity (measured by V-SD, IIV):**
The basal ganglia store procedural motor programs — learned sequences of movement. In AD, basal ganglia circuits are progressively impaired. What once was an automatic, effortful-free action (finger tapping) becomes cortically supervised, meaning movement must be consciously controlled each time. Cortically supervised movement is inherently less regular → higher V-SD and IIV.

**Rhythm Disruption (measured by tap interval variability):**
The cerebellum maintains timing and rhythm. Cerebellar circuits are disrupted as AD spreads via connected cortical regions. Loss of cerebellar timing manifests as inability to maintain a steady tapping rhythm.

**Bimanual Decoupling (measured by phase coherence):**
Independent bimanual control requires the supplementary motor area (SMA) and interhemispheric communication through the corpus callosum. AD disrupts interhemispheric connectivity, making it harder to execute independent bilateral movements, measurable as phase desynchronization.

**Spatial Accuracy Degradation (measured by endpoint error):**
The posterior parietal cortex integrates sensory information (proprioception + vision) to guide precise limb movements. In AD, this region is among the earliest affected by amyloid plaques, degrading the sensory-motor integration needed for spatial precision.

**Non-Dominant Hand Sensitivity:**
The non-dominant hand requires greater cerebellar involvement for fine motor tasks (less automatized than the dominant hand). Since the cerebellum's contribution to movement is more variable and effort-dependent for the non-dominant hand, cognitive impairment is more visible there.

## 8. Limitations of the Current System

Being rigorous about what the system **cannot** do:

1. **No force measurement.** Grip strength is the strongest single predictor (20% AD risk reduction per SD). The camera cannot measure grip force. Would require a paired dynamometer.

2. **Z-axis accuracy.** MediaPipe's depth (`z`) is estimated from 2D monocular vision, not true stereo depth. It is less accurate than x/y. A stereo camera or depth camera (e.g. Intel RealSense) would yield much more reliable 3D data.

3. **Not a clinical diagnostic tool.** This system requires clinical validation, standardized test protocols, normative databases across age/sex/handedness groups, and regulatory approval before any diagnostic use.

4. **Environmental sensitivity.** Lighting, background clutter, and camera distance affect landmark accuracy. Clinical use would require standardized setup.

5. **Simple pegboard not replicated.** The Purdue and nine-hole pegboard involve physical object manipulation and tactile feedback — aspects the camera cannot measure. These tests predicted the 28% risk increase per score in the Darweesh study.

6. **No baseline.** Single-session data is hard to interpret without individual longitudinal baselines, since hand function varies widely between people. The most powerful use case is **repeated measurement over time** to detect decline from a personal baseline.

## 9. Roadmap: From Current System to Diagnostic Tool

### Stage 1 — Feature Extraction Module (Near Term)
Extend `hand_tracking.py` to output not just raw landmark coordinates but computed metrics:
- Frame-by-frame velocity per landmark
- Running IIV calculation on finger-tap event stream
- V-SD over configurable windows
- Left/right hand separation (already implemented)

### Stage 2 — Structured Test Protocol Mode
Add a test session mode that:
1. Guides patient through the 6 tests above (voice/visual prompts)
2. Records raw landmark time series per test
3. Exports CSV/JSON with timestamps and all landmark data

### Stage 3 — Metric Computation Pipeline
Post-session analysis script that:
1. Computes all biomarkers (IIV, V-SD, jerk, spatial accuracy, bimanual coherence)
2. Outputs a structured report per session
3. Compares against previous sessions (longitudinal tracking)

### Stage 4 — Normative Database
Collect data from healthy subjects across age groups to build:
- Age-adjusted norms for each metric
- Z-score calculation per patient relative to norm
- Alerting thresholds based on the published statistical thresholds

### Stage 5 — Validation Study
Pair the system with:
- Clinical MMSE/MoCA assessments
- Standard neuropsychological testing
- Longitudinal follow-up
Validate which computed metrics correlate most strongly with MMSE scores in the system's own data.

## 11. Conclusion

The systematic review provides robust, multi-study evidence that hand function — particularly the *quality and consistency* of fine motor movements — degrades measurably before and during Alzheimer's disease onset. The key insight is not that AD patients move slowly, but that they move **irregularly**: the loss of automatic motor control is the primary signal.

The 3D hand tracking system built around `hand_tracking.py` is exceptionally well-positioned to capture these signals. It already:
- Captures 21 3D landmarks at real-time framerate
- Distinguishes left from right hand
- Streams structured data over UDP for downstream processing

With the addition of:
- Structured test protocols (guided finger tapping, sequential tasks, bimanual tasks)
- Feature extraction computing IIV, V-SD, jerk, and spatial accuracy
- Longitudinal data storage and comparison
- Normative baselines

...the system could constitute a **non-invasive, low-cost, accessible screening tool** for early cognitive decline — precisely the application the paper's authors call for: *"highly usable and accessible as grip strength, measurable in the community without expensive equipment or special facilities."*

The 3D camera is, in many respects, more capable than the instruments used in the studies reviewed. A digitizing tablet cannot capture 3D depth. A magnetic finger sensor captures only one fingertip. A light-beam tapper captures one binary event stream. This system captures **all 21 joints on both hands in three dimensions simultaneously** at video framerate — a superset of most clinical instruments reviewed.

The missing piece is not hardware capability. It is **clinical validation** — the structured protocols, longitudinal data, and normative reference databases needed to interpret a given patient's metrics as meaningful signal. That is the work ahead.
